Using the UX Double Diamond Process to Help Rebrand A Non-Profit

When the political tides turned after the 2020 presidential election, Democracy Counts found that its website's once-effective rallying cry was suddenly a political statement the non-partisan organization didnâ€™t want to make. The infant non-profit group also realized they didn't know who their users (or target audience) were.



To change this, I set out with the goal to make the website better reflect the group's non-partisan mission statement and products (two no-cost apps for recording local election data on your phone). I also wanted to prioritize responsiveness and accessibility on the site because early research indicated that the site's readership skewed older.

Double Diamond Process

Even before the user feedback, there were a couple of things I knew we needed to address. The website's purple color scheme was confusing because it didn't reflect the political nature of the site. Further, it didn't match the coloring of the organization's apps or logos, so the products lacked a cohesive brand identity.

I authored two surveys: one for the internal staff (to determine where to start) and one for the email newsletter list, which I was fortunate to acquire. I used Google Forms for the surveys. The organization also shared its Google Analytics account to provide insight on website traffic patterns, which was immensely helpful in creating the personas and journey map.

I started with a paper affinity diagram but digitized it for clarity (to the right). Future affinity diagrams tracked direct quotations from users as well as user desires we had not considered, such as wanting to start their own election integrity groups locally.

Eventually, wireframes and working prototypes on Adobe Photoshop and Adobe XD brought the idea of the new website to life. Now a developer will help the design go live!
